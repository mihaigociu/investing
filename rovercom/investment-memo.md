# Rover Group — Investment Memo

*A value-investing (Buffett-style) assessment. Retrospective, because Blackstone took Rover private at $11.00/share in February 2024. Every figure below is drawn from Rover's SEC filings and the merger proxy (DEFM14A); see the companion files for the underlying data.*

---

## Verdict in one line

**Rover was a genuinely wonderful business — a profitable, net-cash, category-leading marketplace with a real moat — but it was *not* available at a value-investing price. At $11 it was priced for a decade of flawless execution, offering no margin of safety. The bargain existed in 2022 (~$3–5), not at the buyout.**

Great business ≠ great investment. Price is what separates the two, and this memo is the story of that gap.

---

## 1. The business — and why it's easy to understand

Rover runs an online marketplace that connects pet owners with independent sitters and dog walkers: boarding, house-sitting, drop-in visits, day care, and walking. It owns no kennels and employs no sitters. It simply takes a **~22% cut** (its "take rate") of everything booked through the platform.

The scale of what flows through it is the first thing to grasp. In 2023, roughly **$1.05 billion** of bookings (Gross Booking Value) passed through Rover; Rover kept **~$231 million** of that as revenue. The rest went to the sitters. This is an asset-light, capital-light model — the kind Buffett prizes, because growth doesn't require pouring cash into factories or inventory.

It clears the **circle-of-competence** test easily: you can explain in one sentence how it makes money (a fee on each booking), why customers use it (trust, insurance, reviews, convenience versus a stranger from Craigslist), and how it could fail (customers and sitters going around it to avoid the fee). That last point — **disintermediation** — is the single most important risk, and we return to it below.

## 2. The moat — real, and the numbers prove it

Rover's competitive advantage rests on a **local network effect**: more sitters in a city means more choice and availability for owners, which draws more owners, which draws more sitters. Layer on a trusted **brand**, **switching costs** (your reviews, booking history, and trusted sitters live on Rover), and expensive-to-replicate **trust-and-safety infrastructure** (background checks, the Rover Guarantee, insurance, 24/7 support), and you have a defensible position as the clear category leader.

The most convincing evidence isn't a slide — it's the market price of its nearest public peer. **Wag! (Nasdaq: PET)**, the #2 pure-play, did ~$84M of revenue in 2023, was barely break-even on EBITDA, carried net debt, and was shrinking. It traded at roughly **1x revenue**. Rover — growing ~33%, ~20% EBITDA margins, net cash — commanded roughly **9x revenue**. That ~9x-versus-1x chasm between the leader and the runner-up is the market pricing the moat directly. Wag! is the value trap (cheap for a reason); Rover is the quality compounder you had to pay up for.

Is the moat **widening or eroding**? The bull case is scale and improving retention. The honest bear case is disintermediation and a take rate that management planned to *push from ~22% to 27.5%* — squeezing more from each booking is exactly the kind of move that can eventually drive users off-platform. The moat is real; its durability is the open question.

## 3. Management and capital allocation

On the evidence, management ran the business well: revenue nearly doubled from 2021 to 2022 and grew another ~33% in 2023, while Adjusted EBITDA margin climbed from ~11% to ~20% — textbook operating leverage. They kept the balance sheet pristine (**~$230M net cash, no meaningful debt**) and **returned cash through buybacks**, repurchasing ~9 million shares for ~$49M in 2023 at prices (~$5–9) well below the eventual $11 buyout — value-accretive in hindsight.

Two caveats a careful investor keeps in view. First, **stock-based compensation** (~$22M/yr) is large and is added back in the "Adjusted EBITDA" the company likes to headline; treated honestly as the real cost it is, economic profit is materially lower. Second, the SPAC route to going public in 2021 **more than doubled the share count** (~89M → ~182M) — heavy dilution that every per-share figure must reckon with.

## 4. Financial strength and true earnings power

The quality signals are strong: high gross margins, an asset-light model with only ~$7–9M of annual capex, favorable working capital, net cash, and a genuine crossing into GAAP profitability during 2023.

But **owner earnings** — Buffett's measure of the real, sustainable cash an owner could extract — were modest. Backing SBC and capex out of ~$47M Adjusted EBITDA yields roughly **$15–22M**. The primary source confirms this almost exactly: management's own plan pegged **2023 unlevered free cash flow at $18M**. That is the number that matters, and it is small next to a ~$2.3B price tag — which is the whole valuation problem in a nutshell.

The one real weakness for a philosophy that wants a decade of proof: Rover had **only ~3 years of public financials, one of them COVID-distorted**. You are extrapolating from a short, noisy record.

## 5. Valuation and the "$11 test"

Here is where the business and the price part ways.

At $11.00, Rover was valued at ~$2.3B in equity (~$2.07B enterprise value) — about **9x revenue, ~44x Adjusted EBITDA, and over 100x owner earnings**. Those are growth multiples, not value multiples.

We don't have to argue about it, because the merger proxy hands us the answer key. **Both** of Rover's investment banks valued it against the offer:

| Method | Implied value/share | Where $11 sits |
|---|---|---|
| Goldman Sachs — DCF | $7.87 – $11.41 | near the top |
| Centerview — DCF | $7.40 – $11.25 | at the top |
| Centerview — comps (EV/Revenue) | $6.55 – $9.25 | **above** the range |
| Centerview — comps (EV/Adj. EBITDA) | $8.45 – $10.25 | **above** the range |

And here is what the DCFs had to assume to reach even the *top* of their ranges — management's November 2023 plan:

- Revenue compounding at **~15% for a full decade** ($231M → $961M by 2033),
- Take rate rising from **~22% to 27.5%**,
- Adjusted EBITDA margin **doubling from ~20% to ~42%**,
- Unlevered FCF growing from **$18M to $264M** (a ~31% CAGR),
- discounted at a **high 12.5–14.5% WACC** with a **generous 4–6% perpetuity growth**,
- and **no allowance for any recession** over ten years.

Stack every one of those optimistic assumptions and the banks' models still only reach ~$11.25–$11.41 at the ceiling. Strip out even a little of that optimism — a recession, take-rate resistance, margins settling at 30% instead of 42% — and intrinsic value falls into the **high single digits**, right where the market-based comps already sat.

**The conclusion is unambiguous: at $11 there was essentially no margin of safety.** You would have been paying the full price of a best-case decade. A value investor's required discount simply wasn't on offer.

## 6. What could go wrong (the pre-mortem)

- **Disintermediation** — repeat customers booking their trusted sitter off-platform to dodge the fee; the structural marketplace killer, and the plan's rising take rate makes the incentive worse.
- **Discretionary demand** — boarding is tied to travel; a downturn hits it, and the plan assumed none.
- **Take-rate ceiling** — the growth story leans on charging more per booking, which has a natural limit before supply and demand walk away.
- **Regulatory** — worker-classification (are sitters contractors?), liability for pet harm, local licensing.
- **Short, COVID-distorted history** — thin evidence for a forever-hold thesis.

## 7. Bottom line

Rover passes the Buffett *quality* tests convincingly: understandable, moaty, high-return, net-cash, decently managed, with a secular tailwind behind pet ownership. If the only question were *"is this a wonderful business?"* the answer is yes.

But value investing asks a second question — *"at this price, with a margin of safety?"* — and there the answer at $11 is no. The take-private price was the top of a bullish plan, blessed by two banks whose own models couldn't justify much more. That a sophisticated buyer like **Blackstone** paid it makes sense on *their* terms: leverage, operational levers, and a multi-year hold to a larger exit. It does not make sense for a public shareholder buying common stock and demanding a cushion against being wrong.

**The value opportunity in Rover was real, but it was in the market's fear of 2022 (~$3–5/share), not in the certainty of the 2024 buyout.** The enduring lesson from this case study is the one Buffett repeats: *the quality of the business and the attractiveness of the investment are two different questions, and price is what connects them.*
