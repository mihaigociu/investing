# Buffett Scorecard — Rover Group (final verdict)

Scored 1–5 (1 = clear fail, 5 = excellent). A single hard "fail" on Moat, Management honesty, or Circle of Competence vetoes a buy regardless of price — **and so does a fail on margin of safety (#10), which is the decisive line here.**

> **These are my draft scores with reasoning — argue with them.** They're filled from the verified analysis in the companion files. The judgment should ultimately be yours; treat this as a starting position to push against.

**Sub-scores that tell the story:**
- **Business quality (items 1–9): 34/45 → strong.** This *is* a wonderful business.
- **Investment attractiveness at $11 (item 10): 1/5 → poor.** No margin of safety.

## Verdict

- **Is Rover a *wonderful business*?** **Yes.** Simple, moaty, high-return, net-cash, well-run, with a tailwind. It clears the quality bar clearly.
- **Was it a *wonderful investment* at $11?** **No.** The take-private price was the top of an optimistic plan; a value investor's required discount wasn't on offer.
- **Intrinsic value range:** roughly **$7–11** on the banks' own methods; **high single digits** once you haircut the aggressive take-rate/margin assumptions. Call a conservative central estimate **~$8–9**.
- **My buy price (with a ~35% margin of safety on ~$8.5 intrinsic value):** **~$5.50 or below.**
- **The $11 verdict:** **rich** — a full, growth-priced deal, not a value entry.

## Bottom line (plainly)

> I would happily have *owned* Rover for ten years — as a business, it's the kind Buffett hunts for. But I would not have *bought* it at $11, because that price already assumed a flawless decade and left no room to be wrong. The value window was the 2022 market fear (~$3–5/share), when the identical business traded at a fraction of the buyout price. Blackstone's $11 confirms the smart money saw durable cash flows here — but a PE buyer underwrites leverage, operational levers, and a planned exit that a common-stock value investor cannot. **The single lesson: a great business and a great investment are different questions, and at $11 only the first was true.**
