# Glossary / Annex — Plain-English Guide to the Terms

Every piece of jargon used across this folder, explained simply, with a Rover example where it helps. Skim it once, then use it as a lookup. Terms are grouped by theme, not alphabetized, so related ideas sit together.

---

## A. The big picture — value investing

**Value investing**
Buying a piece of a business (a share) for *less than it's actually worth*, then holding for years while the value is realized. Popularized by Benjamin Graham and made famous by **Warren Buffett**. The opposite of trading on price momentum or hype.

**Intrinsic value**
What a business is *really* worth, based on the cash it will generate over its life — regardless of today's stock price. The whole game is estimating this and comparing it to the price. You can't calculate it precisely; you estimate a *range*.

**Margin of safety**
The gap between intrinsic value and the price you pay. If you think a business is worth $10 and you pay $6, your margin of safety is 40%. It's your protection against being wrong. Buffett/Graham insist on buying well *below* intrinsic value — never at it.

**Moat (economic moat)**
A durable competitive advantage that protects a business from competitors — like a moat around a castle. Examples: a strong brand, network effects, high switching costs. A wide moat lets a company stay profitable for decades. *Rover's potential moat: it's the largest pet-sitting marketplace, so it has the most sitters and the most customers, which reinforces itself.*

**Network effect**
When a product gets *more valuable as more people use it*. More pet sitters on Rover → more choice for owners → more owners join → more sitters join. A powerful, hard-to-copy moat for marketplaces. (Key nuance for Rover: it's **local** — lots of sitters in Seattle doesn't help a customer in Miami.)

**Switching costs**
The hassle/cost of leaving a product for a competitor. On Rover: your booking history, reviews, trusted sitters, and insurance coverage make you reluctant to switch. Higher switching costs = stickier customers = wider moat.

**Circle of competence**
The set of businesses *you personally understand well enough* to judge. Buffett's rule: only invest inside it. If you can't explain how a company makes money and how it could fail, it's outside your circle — skip it.

**Disintermediation ("platform leakage")**
When users bypass the middleman. For Rover: an owner finds a sitter they trust *through* Rover, then books directly next time to avoid the service fee. This is the #1 threat to any marketplace's moat, because it erodes repeat revenue.

## B. The income statement — are they making money?

**Revenue (a.k.a. "the top line")**
The total money a company earns from its core business in a period. *Rover 2023: ~$231M.* Note: for Rover this is the **service fee**, not the total value of bookings (see GBV below).

**GBV — Gross Booking Value**
The *total* dollar value of everything booked through the marketplace, before Rover takes its cut. *Rover 2023: ~$1.05B of bookings flowed through the platform.* Rover's **revenue** is only the fee it keeps on top of that.

**Take rate**
The percentage of GBV that the marketplace keeps as revenue. `Take rate = Revenue ÷ GBV`. *Rover: ~22%* (it keeps ~$22 of every $100 booked). Watch it: raising the take rate boosts revenue but, pushed too far, drives sitters/owners off the platform.

**Gross profit / gross margin**
What's left of revenue after the *direct* costs of delivering the service. `Gross margin = gross profit ÷ revenue`, as a %. Higher = more efficient. Asset-light marketplaces like Rover have high gross margins.

**Contribution margin**
Revenue minus the variable costs tied to each booking (e.g. payment processing, support). What each incremental booking "contributes" toward fixed costs and profit.

**Operating income (operating profit)**
Profit from the core business after *all* operating costs (marketing, salaries, tech, admin) but *before* interest and taxes. Shows whether the actual business — ignoring financing — makes money.

**Net income (a.k.a. "the bottom line," "earnings")**
The final profit after *everything* — all costs, interest, and taxes. If negative, it's a **net loss**. *Rover: net losses in 2021 ($64M) and 2022 ($22M), turning positive in 2023.* This is the "E" in the P/E ratio.

**EBITDA**
**E**arnings **B**efore **I**nterest, **T**axes, **D**epreciation, and **A**mortization. A measure of operating profitability that strips out financing (interest), tax, and non-cash accounting charges (see D&A). Popular because it approximates cash operating profit — but it can *flatter* a company by ignoring real costs.

**Adjusted EBITDA**
EBITDA with *further* items added back — most importantly **stock-based compensation** (see below), plus one-off/"non-recurring" costs. Companies love this metric because it looks the best. **Treat it with suspicion**: it often hides real costs. *Rover 2023: ~$47M adjusted EBITDA — but this adds back ~$22M of stock comp that is a genuine cost to shareholders.*

**GAAP**
**G**enerally **A**ccepted **A**ccounting **P**rinciples — the standardized, audited US accounting rulebook that all public companies must follow. "GAAP net income" = profit measured the official, conservative way. When a company emphasizes "adjusted" or "non-GAAP" numbers that look much better than GAAP, ask *why* — the gap is where the bodies are buried.

**SBC — Stock-Based Compensation**
Paying employees in shares/options instead of (or on top of) cash. It doesn't cost cash *today*, so "adjusted" metrics add it back — but it's a **real cost to you as a shareholder** because it creates new shares and shrinks your ownership (see dilution). Buffett insists on treating SBC as the expense it is. *Rover: ~$19M (2022), ~$22M/yr (2023) — large relative to profit.*

**Operating leverage**
When revenue grows faster than costs, so margins expand as the company scales. *Rover showed this: revenue grew while EBITDA margin climbed from ~11% to ~20%.* A sign of a scalable business model.

**Seasonality**
Predictable within-year swings. *Rover earns more around holidays and summer (people travel → need pet boarding).* Don't judge the business off one quarter.

**Cyclicality / discretionary demand**
Whether demand rises and falls with the economy. "Discretionary" spending (nice-to-have) gets cut in recessions; "non-discretionary" (must-have) holds up. A key question for Rover: do people still pay for pet care in a downturn?

## C. The balance sheet & cash flow — how sturdy are they?

**Balance sheet**
A snapshot of what a company *owns* (assets) and *owes* (liabilities) at a point in time. The difference is shareholders' equity.

**Asset-light**
A business that doesn't need to own lots of expensive physical stuff to grow. *Rover owns no kennels and employs no sitters — it's just the software platform connecting people.* Asset-light businesses can grow cheaply and earn high returns on capital.

**Cash & equivalents / investments**
The company's liquid money and near-cash holdings. A big cash pile is a cushion. *Rover had ~$230M in cash and investments and almost no debt.*

**Debt**
Money the company has borrowed and must repay, usually with interest. High debt is risky in downturns. **Net cash** = more cash than debt (a strong, safe position — Rover's situation). **Net debt** = more debt than cash (riskier — Wag!'s situation).

**Operating cash flow (OCF)**
The actual cash generated by running the business (as opposed to accounting profit, which includes non-cash items). Cash is harder to fake than earnings.

**Capex — Capital Expenditures**
Cash spent on long-lived assets. For Rover, this is mostly *capitalized software development*, not factories — so it's small (~$7–9M/yr).

**Maintenance capex**
The portion of capex needed just to *keep the business running as-is* (vs. capex spent to *grow*). Buffett subtracts maintenance capex to find true owner earnings.

**FCF — Free Cash Flow**
The cash left over after capex: `FCF = operating cash flow − capex`. The money genuinely available to return to shareholders or reinvest. A cleaner measure of value creation than accounting earnings.

**Working capital**
The cash tied up in day-to-day operations (money owed to you, money you owe, timing of payments). Marketplaces can have *favorable* working capital — they briefly hold customers' booking money before paying it out.

**D&A — Depreciation & Amortization**
A non-cash accounting charge that spreads the cost of an asset over its useful life. *Depreciation* = physical assets; *amortization* = intangible assets (like software or acquired brands). It reduces reported profit but isn't a cash outflow in that period — which is why it's added back in EBITDA and cash-flow analysis.

**Dilution**
When new shares are created (via stock comp, acquisitions, or fundraising), each existing share owns a smaller slice of the company. *Rover's share count jumped from ~89M to ~182M around its 2021 SPAC deal — heavy dilution.* Always track profit *per share*, not just total profit.

**Shares outstanding (basic vs. diluted)**
The number of shares that exist. **Basic** = shares currently issued. **Diluted** = basic + shares that *could* be created from options, warrants, and RSUs. Use **diluted** for conservative valuation — it reflects your true future ownership slice.

## D. Returns & quality metrics

**ROE — Return on Equity**
`Net income ÷ shareholders' equity`. How much profit the company generates on the owners' money. Higher and consistent = better.

**ROIC — Return on Invested Capital**
`Operating profit (after tax) ÷ capital invested in the business`. The single best measure of business quality: how much profit it squeezes from every dollar put to work. **High, sustainable ROIC is the quantitative fingerprint of a moat.** (Compute it *excluding excess cash*, or a big cash pile distorts it.)

**Owner earnings**
Buffett's preferred profit measure — roughly the *real, sustainable cash* an owner could pull out each year without harming the business:
`Owner earnings ≈ net income + non-cash charges (D&A) − maintenance capex − stock-based comp ± working-capital needs`.
More conservative and realistic than EBITDA or adjusted EBITDA. *For Rover we estimated ~$15–22M — small next to its ~$2.3B price tag, which is why $11/share was "priced for growth."*

## E. Valuation — what's it worth, and is the price fair?

**DCF — Discounted Cash Flow**
The core valuation method. You (1) project the company's future cash flows for ~10 years, (2) add a "terminal value" for everything after, and (3) **discount** them back to today's dollars (because a dollar in the future is worth less than a dollar now). The total = your estimate of intrinsic value. Sensitive to your assumptions — garbage in, garbage out. *Centerview's DCF for Rover produced a range of $7.40–$11.25/share.*

**Discount rate**
The annual rate used to shrink future cash to present value — it reflects both the time value of money and risk. Higher risk → higher discount rate → lower value today. Often ~9–10% for a stable business. (Bankers use a formula called **WACC**, the weighted average cost of capital.)

**Terminal value**
The estimated value of all cash flows *beyond* the explicit forecast period (e.g. year 10 onward), usually assuming modest steady growth forever. Often the largest single chunk of a DCF — and the most assumption-dependent.

**Terminal growth rate**
The assumed perpetual growth rate after the forecast period — kept modest (2–3%), roughly economy-wide growth, because nothing grows fast forever.

**Reverse DCF**
Instead of guessing future growth to find value, you start from *today's price* and solve for *what growth the price already assumes*. Then you judge whether that implied growth is realistic or heroic. The most honest sanity-check — it sidesteps forecasting bias. *At $11, Rover's price implied very high sustained growth.*

**Enterprise Value (EV)**
The value of the *whole business* regardless of how it's financed: `EV = market value of equity + debt − cash`. You subtract cash because a buyer effectively gets that cash back. Used in EV-based multiples so you can compare companies with different debt levels fairly. *Rover at $11: ~$2.3B equity − ~$0.23B net cash ≈ ~$2.07B EV.*

**Market capitalization ("market cap")**
The market value of just the equity: `share price × shares outstanding`. What all the shares are collectively worth. *Rover at $11 ≈ $2.3B.*

**Valuation multiple**
A shorthand ratio comparing price to a fundamental. Lets you compare companies quickly and against history. The common ones:

- **P/E — Price-to-Earnings** = share price ÷ earnings per share (or market cap ÷ net income). "How many years of current profit am I paying?" High P/E = expensive or high-growth-expected.
- **EV/Revenue (EV/Sales)** = enterprise value ÷ revenue. Used for fast-growing or barely-profitable firms. *Rover at $11 ≈ 9x; Wag! ≈ 1x — that gap signals Rover's quality.*
- **EV/EBITDA** = enterprise value ÷ EBITDA. A common "how expensive is this business" gauge. *Rover at $11 ≈ 44x — rich.*
- **P/FCF — Price-to-Free-Cash-Flow** = market cap ÷ free cash flow. How much you pay per dollar of actual cash generated.

"**Rich**"/"**expensive**" = a high multiple (a lot paid per dollar of value). "**Cheap**" = a low multiple. But **cheap can be a trap** if the business is deteriorating (see value trap).

**Comps (comparable companies analysis)**
Valuing a company by looking at the multiples of *similar* companies. *We compared Rover to Wag! (PET).* A market-based cross-check on a DCF.

**Precedent transactions**
Valuing a company by what buyers paid for *similar companies in past M&A deals*. Another cross-check, usually including an acquisition premium.

**Value trap**
A stock that looks cheap on the numbers but is cheap *for a good reason* — a declining business. *Wag! at ~1x sales looks cheap, but it's shrinking and barely breaks even.* Buffett's warning: a fair company at a wonderful price is still a poor investment.

## F. Deal & filing terminology

**Take-private**
When a public company is bought and removed from the stock market, becoming privately owned. *Blackstone took Rover private at $11/share in Feb 2024, so its shares no longer trade.*

**Acquisition premium**
The extra amount an acquirer pays above the pre-deal share price to persuade shareholders to sell. *Rover's $11 was ~30%+ above the undisturbed price and ~61% above the 90-day average.*

**Private equity (PE)**
Firms (like **Blackstone**) that buy whole companies — often using borrowed money (leverage) — improve or grow them, and sell them later for a profit. They value businesses differently than a buy-and-hold stock investor (they use debt and plan an exit).

**SPAC — Special Purpose Acquisition Company**
A shell company that raises money in an IPO, then merges with a real business to bring it public — an alternative to a traditional IPO. *Rover went public via a SPAC (Nebula Caravel) in 2021.* SPAC deals often come with **warrants** and heavy dilution.

**Warrants**
Securities giving the holder the right to buy shares at a set price later. Common in SPAC deals; they can dilute existing shareholders when exercised.

**Fairness opinion**
An independent investment bank's formal judgment that a deal's price is "fair, from a financial point of view" to shareholders. *Centerview provided Rover's.* It includes the valuation analyses (DCF, comps) — a useful, if deal-motivated, "answer key."

**SEC filings you'll see referenced:**
- **10-K** — the audited **annual** report. The most detailed and reliable document. The backbone of any analysis.
- **10-Q** — the **quarterly** report (less detail, unaudited).
- **8-K** — a report of a **major event** (earnings release, a deal, a CEO change) filed as it happens.
- **DEFM14A / PREM14A** — the **merger proxy** sent to shareholders to vote on a deal. Contains the fairness opinion, management projections, and deal background — *gold for our "$11 test."* ("DEF" = definitive/final; "PRE" = preliminary.)
- **DEF 14A** — the annual **proxy statement** (executive pay, board, shareholder votes).
- **S-4** — the registration/proxy document for a merger (used in Rover's original SPAC deal).
- **EDGAR** — the SEC's free public database where all these filings live (sec.gov).

**CAGR — Compound Annual Growth Rate**
The smoothed average yearly growth rate over a multi-year period. Lets you say "revenue grew X% per year on average" even if individual years bounced around.
