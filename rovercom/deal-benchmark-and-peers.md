# The $11 Answer Key & Peer Comps — Rover Group

Two independent checks on our own valuation work:
1. **The banker's fairness opinion** (Centerview Partners) — the "answer key" the board relied on to bless $11.00.
2. **Wag! (Nasdaq: PET)** — Rover's closest public peer, for a market-based comps read.

---

## Part 1 — The fairness opinion (the answer key)

Rover's board used **two** investment banks: **Goldman Sachs** (lead advisor) and **Centerview Partners** (delivered the fairness opinion). Both valued Rover several ways and compared each to the **$11.00** cash offer. Here is what they got (implied equity value **per share**) — *all figures now pulled directly from the DEFM14A*:

| Bank / methodology | Implied per-share range | Where $11.00 sits |
|---|---|---|
| **Goldman Sachs — Discounted Cash Flow** (WACC 12.5–14.5%; perpetuity growth 4–6%) | **$7.87 – $11.41** | near the **top** |
| **Centerview — Discounted Cash Flow** (Nov-2023 plan, UFCF 2023–2032) | **$7.40 – $11.25** | at the **top** (~98% of ceiling) |
| **Centerview — Selected Companies (EV/Revenue)** | **$6.55 – $9.25** | **above** the top |
| **Centerview — Selected Companies (EV/Adj. EBITDA)** | **$8.45 – $10.25** | **above** the top |

**Premium framing:** $11.00 was ~**61%** above the 90-trading-day volume-weighted average price (through 28 Nov 2023) and roughly **30%+** above the undisturbed close.

> Note the **high discount rate**: Goldman used a 12.5–14.5% WACC (vs. the ~9–10% a value investor might apply to a stable blue-chip). That reflects Rover's risk/growth profile — and even so, $11 only reaches the *top* of the range, and only with a generous 4–6% perpetuity growth assumption (implying a rich 6.5x–10.7x terminal EV/EBITDA exit multiple).

### What this tells a value investor

- **$11 was a full-to-rich price, not a bargain.** Only the DCF — and only when built on *management's own optimistic long-term plan* — reaches ~$11. **Every market-based comp says fair value was ~$9–10 or below.** A Graham/Buffett investor demanding a margin of safety would get **none** at $11; you'd be paying the top of the banker's most generous method.
- **This confirms our independent finding** (`financials-actuals.md` / `valuation-worksheet.md`): on ~$15–22M current owner earnings and ~44x EBITDA, $11 was priced for growth. The banker's numbers land in the same place from the other direction.
- **The DCF is the swing factor.** If you believe management's decade-long plan (continued ~20%+ growth *and* margins expanding toward ~30%), you get to ~$11. If you haircut those assumptions even modestly, intrinsic value drops into the **high-single digits** — exactly the comps range. *That gap is the entire debate.*

> **Verdict on the deal:** For **Blackstone** (leverage, synergies, a multi-year hold to a larger exit), $11 was a rational entry on a quality asset. For a **public value investor buying common stock with a margin of safety**, $11 offered no cushion — the opportunity was earlier and cheaper (Rover's **2022 lows of ~$3–5**), not at the buyout. Great business; full price.

### The actual management projections (November 2023 long-term plan)

Extracted directly from the DEFM14A — the numbers the bankers discounted to justify $11. Dollars in millions:

| Fiscal year | 2023E | 2024E | 2025E | 2026E | 2027E | 2028E | 2029E | 2030E | 2031E | 2032E | 2033E |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| **Revenue** | 231 | 278 | 338 | 403 | 473 | 545 | 619 | 698 | 782 | 871 | 961 |
| **Adjusted EBITDA** | 50 | 75 | 105 | 136 | 172 | 208 | 242 | 280 | 322 | 363 | 404 |
| **Unlevered FCF** (ex-NOLs) | 18 | 35 | 54 | 75 | 99 | 123 | 148 | 175 | 204 | 234 | 264 |

**The assumptions baked into that plan** (this is what you're really buying at $11):
- Revenue **CAGR ~15%** for a full decade (2023→2033).
- **U.S. take rate rising from ~22% to 27.5%** by 2032–2033 — i.e. Rover keeps a *bigger* cut of each booking (a lever with real disintermediation risk).
- **Adjusted EBITDA margin expanding from ~20% to ~42%** by 2033 — a doubling, via take-rate gains, lower cancellations, and operating leverage.
- New-customer-acquisition CAGR ~8%; average booking value +3%/yr; cancellations improving to 11%.
- **No** allowance for macro/recession shocks.

> **Validation of our own work:** management's **2023 UFCF of $18M** lands right inside the **~$15–22M owner-earnings** range we estimated independently in `valuation-worksheet.md`. Good — the bottoms-up and the primary source agree.

## Part 2 — Wag! (PET) peer comparison

Wag! is the closest US-listed pure-play pet-care marketplace (dog walking, sitting, boarding, plus wellness/insurance-lead-gen). It's a *much* smaller, weaker business — which is exactly why the comparison is instructive.

| Metric (FY2023) | **Rover** | **Wag! (PET)** |
|---|---:|---:|
| Revenue | ~$231M | ~$83.9M |
| Revenue growth YoY | ~+33% | roughly flat → declining |
| Adjusted EBITDA | ~$47M (~20% margin) | ~$0.7M (~1% margin) |
| GAAP net income | positive (est.) | ~$(13.3)M loss |
| Net cash / (debt) | +~$230M net cash | ~$(7)M **net debt** (≈$27M debt vs. ~$20M cash) |
| Shares outstanding | ~181M | ~39.6M |

### Valuation comparison

| Multiple | Rover @ $11 (~$2.07B EV) | Wag! (~$75–80M EV, est.) |
|---|---:|---:|
| EV / Revenue | **~9.0x** | **~0.9–1.0x** |
| EV / Adj. EBITDA | **~44x** | n/m (~110x — EBITDA ~breakeven) |

> Wag!'s EV is **estimated**: ~39.6M shares × ~$1.5–2.0 late-2023 price ≈ $60–80M market cap, + ~$27M debt − ~$20M cash ≈ **~$75–80M EV**. Verify against PET's 10-K; the *magnitude* of the gap is what matters, not the second decimal.

### What the ~9x-vs-~1x gap means

- **The market was not valuing "pet-care marketplaces" richly — it was valuing *Rover specifically* richly.** An ~8–9x revenue-multiple gap between the category leader and the #2 is a **direct market-price signal of moat**: scale, profitability, growth, brand, and two-sided liquidity that Wag! lacks.
- **Wag! is the value trap; Rover is the quality compounder.** Wag! is statistically "cheap" (~1x sales) but shrinking, barely breakeven, and carrying net debt — cheap for good reason. Buffett's whole point: a fair company at a wonderful price is still a poor investment. Rover is the "wonderful company," and the market made you pay up for it.
- **Comps cut both ways for the $11 question.** Rover *deserves* a large premium to Wag! — but "deserves a premium" ≠ "offers a margin of safety." At ~9x sales, Rover's price already capitalized most of its superiority. The quality was real; the discount was not.

---

## Bottom line from both checks

Both the banker's answer key and the peer comps point the same direction as our own analysis:

> **Rover was a genuinely high-quality, moaty, profitable, net-cash marketplace — a "wonderful business."** But at **$11**, it was priced at or above fair value on every method except a management-plan DCF. The value-investing opportunity in Rover existed during the **2022 market fear (~$3–5)**, not at the 2024 take-private. **Great business ≠ great investment; price is what decides.**
