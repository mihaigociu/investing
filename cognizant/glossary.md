# Glossary — Plain-English Annex (Cognizant analysis)

Every term used in this folder, explained for a non-specialist. Two sections: **A. Value-investing terms** (the Buffett toolkit) and **B. IT-services terms** (how to read *this specific* business). If the jargon is new, start here.

---

## A. Value-investing terms

**Value investing** — Buying a stock for meaningfully less than the business is worth ("intrinsic value"), so that even if you're somewhat wrong, you don't lose much. The gap between price and value is the **margin of safety**.

**Intrinsic value** — What a business is *actually worth*, based on the cash it will generate for its owners over its life, discounted back to today. Not the same as the stock price. The whole game is estimating intrinsic value and comparing it to price.

**Margin of safety** — The discount of price to intrinsic value. Buy at 60–70 cents on the dollar so that being wrong still leaves you whole. For a risky/uncertain business you demand a *wider* margin (here, 35–40%).

**Moat** — A durable competitive advantage that protects a company's profits from competitors, the way a moat protects a castle. Sources: switching costs, network effects, brand, cost advantages, scale. A **wide** moat is strong; a **narrow** moat is weak; an **eroding** moat is shrinking (danger). The single best question to test one: *can the company raise prices without losing customers?* (pricing power).

**Pricing power** — The ability to raise prices without losing business. Buffett calls it the most important sign of a good business. Cognizant is *"sticky but price-squeezed"* — customers stay but push prices *down*, i.e., **weak pricing power** = narrow moat.

**Circle of competence** — The set of businesses you understand well enough to judge. Stay inside it. (As an employee, Cognizant is squarely inside yours — with the caveat that inside knowledge can bias as well as inform.)

**Owner earnings** — Buffett's preferred measure of *real* profit: the cash an owner could pull out each year without harming the business. Roughly: net income + non-cash charges − the capital spending needed to maintain the business − real stock-comp cost. For Cognizant, ~$2.48B (~$5.24/share).

**Free cash flow (FCF)** — Cash from operations minus capital expenditures. The cash actually left over. **FCF conversion** = FCF ÷ net income; above 100% (Cognizant ~120%) means reported profit is backed by real cash.

**DCF (Discounted Cash Flow)** — A valuation that projects a company's future cash flows and "discounts" them to today's value (a dollar in 10 years is worth less than a dollar now). Sum of all discounted future cash = intrinsic value.

**Discount rate** — The annual rate used to shrink future cash to present value; reflects risk and the return you require. We use ~10% for Cognizant (higher than a safe blue-chip, to price the AI-disruption risk).

**Terminal value / terminal growth** — In a DCF, the value of all cash flows beyond the explicit forecast (say, year 10+), assuming a steady long-run growth (or decline) rate thereafter.

**Reverse DCF** — Instead of forecasting cash flows to *derive* a value, you take the *current price* and solve for what growth/decline it implies. More honest, because it exposes the market's bet instead of your guesses. Key finding here: ~$43 implies **~3–5%/yr perpetual decline** — so you win unless the business declines faster than that.

**Perpetuity value** — The value of a constant (or steadily growing/declining) cash flow forever = `cash flow ÷ (discount rate − growth rate)`. At a 10% discount and 0% growth, that's 10× the cash flow; Cognizant trades at ~7×, implying negative growth.

**Enterprise value (EV)** — The value of the whole business regardless of how it's financed = market cap + debt − cash. For Cognizant: ~$20.4B market cap − ~$1.34B net cash ≈ **~$19.0B**. Used for EV/Revenue, EV/EBIT, EV/FCF so cash-rich and debt-heavy firms compare fairly.

**Market cap** — Share price × shares outstanding. Cognizant ≈ $43 × ~474M ≈ **$20.4B**.

**Net cash / net debt** — Cash and investments minus total debt. **Net cash** (Cognizant ~$1.34B) means more cash than debt — a fortress balance sheet. Net debt is the reverse.

**P/E ratio** — Price ÷ earnings per share. A rough "years of earnings you're paying." Cognizant ~8x (cheap); its 10-yr median ~18x. **Forward P/E** uses *next year's* expected earnings.

**P/FCF** — Price ÷ free cash flow per share. Like P/E but on cash. Cognizant ~8x vs. ~18x historically.

**GAAP vs. adjusted (non-GAAP)** — **GAAP** = the standardized, audited accounting rules. **Adjusted** = management's version that strips out items it deems one-off (restructuring, acquisition costs, sometimes stock comp). Adjusted numbers flatter results — always check the size of the gap. Cognizant's gap (adj EPS $5.28 vs GAAP $4.56) is modest and mostly stock-comp/amortization — comparatively honest.

**Stock-based compensation (SBC)** — Paying employees in shares instead of (or on top of) cash. It's a *real* cost (it dilutes owners) even though it's non-cash, so owner earnings *subtract* it. Cognizant's is genuinely low (10-K-actual **~$181M, ~0.9% of revenue**) — a quality point, far cleaner than most tech firms and ~10x lighter than Rover's ~10%-of-revenue SBC.

**Dividend yield / payout ratio** — **Yield** = annual dividend ÷ price (Cognizant ~3%). **Payout ratio** = dividends ÷ earnings (Cognizant ~29% — low, so the dividend is safe and has room to grow).

**Buyback (share repurchase)** — A company buying its own shares, shrinking the count so each remaining share owns more of the business. Value-accretive when done *below* intrinsic value. Cognizant is retiring ~3%/yr — accretive at ~8x FCF *if the business doesn't decline*.

**Value trap** — A stock that looks cheap (low P/E, high yield) but is cheap *for a reason* — the business is in permanent decline, so the low price is justified and often falls further. The central risk for Cognizant. The opposite error to overpaying for Rover.

**ROIC / ROE (return on invested capital / equity)** — How much profit the business earns per dollar of capital tied up. High, sustained ROIC is the quantitative fingerprint of a moat. Services firms score high because they need little capital.

**Cyclical vs. structural** — A **cyclical** problem passes with the economic cycle (buy the dip). A **structural** problem is permanent change (don't). The whole Cognizant debate is whether AI is a cyclical headwind or a structural one — you buy the first at a 20% discount, demand 35–40% for the second.

## B. IT-services terms (reading *this* business)

**IT services / outsourcing** — Companies hire a vendor (Cognizant) to build, run, and modernize their technology instead of doing it all in-house. Cognizant provides the people and expertise; the client pays for time and outcomes.

**Labor arbitrage** — The core profit engine: bill the client at a high (onshore-blended) rate, deliver much of the work from lower-cost countries (mostly India), and keep the difference. **This is exactly the spread generative AI threatens to compress** — if AI does the work, you can't bill for the hours.

**Offshore / onshore / nearshore mix** — Where the work is delivered. More **offshore** (low-cost) work = higher margin. The mix is a key margin lever.

**Utilization** — The percentage of billable employees actually billing at any time. Higher utilization = more revenue per head = higher margin. Idle "on the bench" staff drag margins.

**The pyramid** — The staffing structure: many low-cost juniors at the base, fewer expensive seniors at the top. A healthy pyramid keeps blended delivery cost low. AI threatens the *base* of the pyramid (junior/repetitive work) most directly.

**Billable hours / time-and-materials (T&M)** — Contracts where the client pays per hour/per person. **Directly exposed** to AI: fewer hours needed = less revenue. Contrast with:

**Fixed-price / managed services / outcome-based** — Contracts where the client pays a fixed amount for a defined outcome or service level. Here, AI productivity *helps the vendor* — deliver the same outcome with fewer hours and *keep* the saving as margin. **The shift between these two contract types is central to whether AI hurts or helps Cognizant.**

**Bookings / TCV (Total Contract Value)** — The total value of contracts signed, usually reported on a trailing-12-month basis (Cognizant ~$28.4B). A *leading indicator* of future revenue — what clients have committed to, ahead of the work being done and billed.

**Book-to-bill ratio** — Bookings ÷ revenue. Above 1.0 means the company is signing future work faster than it's burning through current work — backlog is growing. Cognizant ~1.3x, historically a positive forward signal. Watch for it dropping below 1.0 (a warning). *Caveat:* TCV can hide *pricing* erosion (more work at lower rates).

**Backlog** — Contracted future revenue not yet recognized. Provides visibility/stability that a transactional business lacks.

**Constant currency (cc)** — Revenue growth with exchange-rate movements stripped out, so you see the *real* underlying growth. Cognizant's 2025 growth was +7.0% reported, +6.4% cc. Always prefer cc (and *organic*) growth to judge the actual business.

**Organic vs. inorganic growth** — **Organic** = growth from the existing business. **Inorganic** = growth bought via acquisitions. Only organic growth reflects the underlying health; acquisitions can mask stagnation.

**Attrition** — The rate at which employees leave (Cognizant's tech-services voluntary attrition ~13.9% TTM). High attrition raises hiring/training costs and can hurt delivery quality; very *low* attrition in a weak job market can mean people are stuck, not loyal.

**Revenue per employee** — Revenue ÷ headcount (~$60K for Cognizant). A rough productivity gauge. If AI lifts productivity *without* gutting revenue, this rises while headcount flattens — a bullish sign. If revenue falls faster than headcount, the reverse.

**Segments** — Cognizant's four industry groups: **Health Sciences**, **Financial Services**, **Products & Resources**, and **Communications, Media & Technology (CMT)**. Different growth and cyclicality; the two largest are Health Sciences and Financial Services.

**Discretionary vs. non-discretionary spend** — **Discretionary** = optional "transformation"/new-project spend, cut first in a downturn (cyclical). **Non-discretionary** = "keep-the-lights-on" run/maintain work clients can't stop paying for (sticky). The mix determines how the business behaves in a recession.

**GenAI cannibalization / disintermediation** — The structural threat: generative AI making developers so productive (or letting clients/AI-native vendors do the work directly) that far fewer billable Cognizant hours are needed. The IT-services analog to a marketplace being "disintermediated." Per the insider view, *already happening today* on real projects — the single biggest swing factor in the whole thesis.
