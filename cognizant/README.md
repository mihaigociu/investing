# Cognizant — Value Investing Analysis

A Warren Buffett–style value investing analysis of **Cognizant Technology Solutions Corp. (Nasdaq: `CTSH`)**.

## The decision this folder is built to answer

> **CTSH trades around ~$43 (July 2026), near a multi-year low — down ~48% year-to-date from ~$83, against a 52-week range of ~$37–$87. Is this a value-investing buy *now*, should I *wait* for a lower price, or is it a *value trap* to avoid entirely?**

This is a **live, forward-looking decision** — the opposite of the Rover case study in the sibling `rovercom/` folder. There, the question was retrospective (was Blackstone's $11 buyout a bargain?) and there was a real buyer's price to grade against. Here, **there is no answer key.** You are the buyer, the market is quoting you a price right now, and the future is genuinely unresolved.

## Why this is the mirror image of Rover — and why that matters

| | **Rover (ROVR)** | **Cognizant (CTSH)** |
|---|---|---|
| Business | Asset-light marketplace, ~22% take rate | Labor-arbitrage IT services (people-on-projects) |
| Quality | Wonderful business | Decent-but-threatened business |
| Price when analyzed | **Rich** (~9x revenue, ~44x EBITDA) | **Cheap** (~0.9x revenue, ~8x FCF, ~8x P/E) |
| The trap to avoid | Overpaying for quality | Underestimating decline ("value trap") |
| Buffett lesson | *A great business ≠ a great investment* | *A cheap price ≠ a margin of safety* |

Rover taught that a wonderful business can be a poor investment if you overpay. Cognizant is the reverse test: **a cheap stock is only a bargain if the cheapness is temporary fear rather than permanent decline.** The entire verdict turns on one question — *is generative AI structurally deflating the IT-services labor model, or is the 2026 crash an overreaction?*

## The insider edge (and its hazard)

This analysis is unusual because the analyst is a **current Cognizant employee**. That insight is woven into `insider-perspective.md` and the scorecard. Used well, it's a real edge on the qualitative questions (moat, morale, whether AI is *actually* cutting billable hours). Used badly, familiarity breeds either bias — over-pessimism from daily frustration, or over-confidence from proximity. Every insider input below is flagged as such and stress-tested, not taken as gospel.

> ⚠️ **Not investment advice, and a conflict to name out loud.** You work here and may hold or receive CTSH stock (RSUs/ESPP). That is a *concentration risk*: your salary and your portfolio would both be exposed to the same company. Factor that in before buying more.

## How to use this

Work the phases **in order** in `value-investing-analysis-plan.md`. Phases 1–3 are qualitative (understand it? moat? management?). Phases 4–5 are quantitative (earnings power, intrinsic value). Phase 6 is the pre-mortem (what makes this a value trap?). Phase 7 is the verdict and the buy/wait decision.

> **Guiding principle (Buffett):** *"It's far better to buy a wonderful company at a fair price than a fair company at a wonderful price."* Cognizant is closer to the second kind. So the discipline flips: the danger isn't overpaying for quality — it's mistaking a melting ice cube for a bargain. **First decide whether the business merely *stagnates* or actually *declines*. Only then does the cheap price become interesting.**
