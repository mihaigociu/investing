# Cognizant — Investment Memo

*A value-investing (Buffett-style) assessment of a live buy/wait decision. CTSH trades ~$43 (July 2026), near a multi-year low, down ~48% YTD from ~$83. Every figure is drawn from Cognizant's filings/press releases and current market data; see the companion files for the underlying detail. The analyst is a Cognizant employee — see `insider-perspective.md` for how that edge (and bias) was handled.*

---

## Verdict in one line

**Cognizant at ~$43 is genuinely cheap — ~8x free cash flow, net cash, a ~3% dividend, buying back ~3% of shares a year, priced by the market for perpetual gentle decline. But it is a *narrow, eroding* moat run by a management the inside view distrusts, with its core labor model under real (not hypothetical) AI pressure. So it's cheap-but-not-yet-safe: WAIT for the low $30s or for hard evidence the revenue line survives the AI transition — and buy only a small starter before then.**

Cheap ≠ margin of safety. Price is what separates a bargain from a value trap, and this memo is the story of which one Cognizant is.

---

## The mirror image of Rover — start here

If you read the `rovercom/` case study, hold it in mind, because Cognizant is its photographic negative:

- **Rover** was a *wonderful business at a rich price* ($11 ≈ 9x sales, 100x owner earnings). The lesson: a great company can be a bad investment if you overpay.
- **Cognizant** is a *fair business at a cheap price* (~$43 ≈ 0.9x sales, ~8x owner earnings). The mirror lesson, also Buffett's: *"a fair company at a wonderful price"* is still not the goal — and if the "fair" is quietly turning into "declining," a cheap price is a trap, not a gift.

Rover's danger was overpaying for quality. Cognizant's danger is **mistaking a melting ice cube for a bargain.** The whole memo is about testing whether the ice is actually melting.

---

## 1. The business — easy to understand, which is the good news

Cognizant sells **IT services**: ~351,600 people (mostly in India-based delivery centers) build, run, and modernize technology for large banks, insurers, healthcare payers, manufacturers, and retailers. The economic engine is **labor arbitrage** — bill the client at an onshore-blended rate, deliver much of the work from lower-cost geographies, keep the spread — amplified by **utilization** (keep people billable) and the **pyramid** (many juniors under fewer seniors).

It clears the **circle-of-competence** test easily, especially for an employee: you can say in a sentence how it makes money (billable people on client projects), why clients use it (switching costs, scale, trusted delivery), and how it fails — **AI making each person so productive that a project needs far fewer billable hours.** That last point is the entire investment question, and we return to it throughout.

The contrast with Rover is instructive: Rover was asset-light and *scaled without adding cost*. Cognizant's growth has historically *required adding people* — a fundamentally lower-quality economic model, and precisely the model AI threatens.

## 2. The moat — real but narrow, and eroding

Cognizant's moat is **switching costs**: once it runs a client's core systems and knows their environment, replacing it is costly and risky. That's genuine, and it's why clients don't just walk — bookings were still ~1.3x book-to-bill and headcount still *grew* in 2025.

But two things make it a **narrow** moat, and the inside view is decisive on both:

- **No pricing power.** Clients are *"sticky but price-squeezed"* — they stay, but they renegotiate rates hard and demand that AI savings be handed back. A moat that defends *volume* but not *price* surrenders its economics over time. Buffett's litmus test — can you raise price? — Cognizant increasingly *fails*.
- **The core model is being cannibalized.** From the delivery floor, GenAI is *"already cannibalizing"* billable hours *today.* This is the Rover-"disintermediation" analog: the structural marketplace/model killer. Outsiders are guessing at this from Accenture's guidance; the insider is watching it happen.

Cognizant has *historically* been the **quality laggard** of ~6 substitutable majors (years of ~2% growth and share losses, lower margin than TCS/Infosys) — though, tellingly, its **+6.4% constant-currency growth in FY2025 was the best of the entire peer group** (Infosys +3.1%, HCLTech +3.9%, Wipro +1.6%, Accenture's FY26 guide +3–4%, and TCS actually −2.4%). So the "deservedly cheapest laggard" story is contradicted by the most recent numbers. Still, the moat verdict is **narrow and eroding** — the classic value-trap profile. Not "no moat" (switching costs are real), but not the widening castle Buffett hunts for.

*One important mitigant, from the actual 10-K contract-mix disclosure (see `contract-mix-and-ai-exposure.md`):* the crude "AI guts the whole labor model" fear overstates the revenue exposure. Only **~43% of revenue is time-and-materials** (billed by the hour — the bucket AI directly deflates), and that bucket is *already* the slowest-growing and losing share. **~47% is fixed-price** (billed for an outcome), where AI efficiency actually *expands Cognizant's margin* — and fixed-price is *gaining* share (+15.5% over two years vs. +2.9% for T&M). So the real fight is less "revenue collapse" and more "does Cognizant *keep* the AI productivity gains on its fixed-price book, or hand them back as lower prices?" — which loops straight back to the "price-squeezed" pricing-power problem. The mix softens the volume bear case; it does not solve the pricing one.

## 3. Management and capital allocation — the decisive soft spot

Credit where due: CEO **Ravi Kumar** (from Infosys, 2023) re-accelerated a stagnant business — +7% revenue in 2025 (best in years), four straight organic-growth quarters, modest margin expansion. Operationally, 2025 was a good year.

But the **honesty / owner-orientation** pillar is where this analysis catches. The inside view is that management is **extracting** — skipping raises for most of the workforce while *"senior management got their share."* Two readings, and an honest memo holds both:

- *As a shareholder*, freezing wages in an uncertain, AI-threatened year is defensible cost discipline, and returning ~$2B via buybacks (at ~8x FCF — value-accretive) plus a raised, well-covered dividend is rational, pro-owner capital allocation. Some of what feels like "extraction" from the employee seat is management doing its job for owners.
- *But* the **asymmetry** — top protected, staff squeezed — is a real candor/partnership red flag, it flatters current margins with a lever that can't be pulled forever, and it risks the very asset (skilled people) that *is* the company. It caps the management-honesty score near veto level.

The right move is to **verify it in the proxy**: is executive comp tied to per-share value and returns on capital, or to revenue and "adjusted" metrics? Are executives *buying* at $43, or only selling vested stock? Until then, treat it as a serious flag, not a settled fact.

## 4. Financial strength and true earnings power — unambiguously strong

This is Cognizant's best pillar. High returns on capital (capital-light: capex ~1% of revenue), **net cash of ~$1.34B** (no solvency risk, survives a long downturn), and — the quality tell — **free cash flow of ~$2.67B, about 120% of net income.** The profit is real cash, not accruals. A confirming detail from the 10-K: **stock-based comp is only ~$181M (~0.9% of revenue)** — Cognizant barely leans on the "adjust it away" games most tech firms play (and it's ~10x cleaner than Rover's SBC as a share of revenue).

**Owner earnings** (Buffett's measure): back the (small) SBC out of FCF and you get **~$2.48B, ~$5.24/share.** Against ~$43, that's **~8.2x owner earnings, a ~12% owner-earnings yield** — the exact opposite of Rover's ~100x. Two caveats from the actual filings: (1) FY2025 **GAAP net income was flat vs. 2024** ($2,230M vs $2,240M) *despite* operating income jumping +$497M — a ~$500M below-the-line (almost certainly tax) item absorbed the gain, which is why GAAP EPS rose only +1% while adjusted rose +11%; normalize for that. (2) Unlike Rover's 3-year, COVID-distorted record, Cognizant has **20+ years of public history through multiple cycles** — real evidence, with one enormous caveat: *the AI era has no precedent in that record.*

## 5. Valuation — cheap on the past; the reverse-DCF tells the real story

Every backward-looking lens screams cheap: P/E ~8x, P/FCF ~8x, EV/revenue ~0.9x, ~3% yield — roughly *half* Cognizant's own 10-year medians and the cheapest of the large peers. But "cheap vs. history" assumes the future rhymes with the past, which is exactly what AI puts in doubt. So the honest tool isn't a multiple — it's the **reverse-DCF**:

> At ~$43 (EV ~$19B, FCF ~$2.5–2.7B ≈ 7x EV/FCF), the market is pricing Cognizant's cash flow to **decline ~3–5% per year, forever.**

That single sentence reframes everything. **You don't need Cognizant to grow. You need it to decline by *less than ~4% a year.*** Flat is a win. Any growth is a big win. The bull doesn't have to claim "AI is no threat" — only "the labor model won't shrink faster than ~4%/yr." The bear has to claim it will — which, per the insider ("already cannibalizing," "price-squeezed"), is a live possibility, not a strawman.

Scenario intrinsic values (owner-earnings DCF, 10% discount):

| Scenario | Assumption | Intrinsic value | vs. ~$43 |
|---|---|---:|---|
| **Bear** | AI deflates the model, ~5–8%/yr decline | **~$30–35** | overpaying |
| **Base** | Stagnation / roughly flat; AI a wash | **~$50–55** | ~15–25% undervalued |
| **Bull** | Growth holds, AI a net tailwind, margins rise | **~$65–75** | deeply undervalued |

The $30–$75 spread is wide *because the AI question is genuinely unresolved* — a tight number here would be false precision. Base ~$52. Demanding a **35–40% margin of safety** (narrow/eroding moat + management flag + *structural* not cyclical threat) gives a **buy price of ~$32–34.**

So at ~$43: **below fair value, above the buy price. Cheap, but not yet a margin of safety.**

## 6. What could go wrong (the pre-mortem)

- **AI structurally deflates the labor model** — the thesis-killer. If revenue turns to sustained *decline*, 8x isn't cheap; it's correct, and it falls further. *(Insider says this is already underway on projects.)*
- **Pricing power keeps eroding** — "sticky but price-squeezed" grinds margins down for years even if revenue holds.
- **Value-trap dynamics** — a cheap multiple that stays cheap while earnings slowly melt; you collect 3% and lose 5%/yr. Indian peers already down ~50% from peaks show it can run further.
- **Talent erosion** — the wage-freeze morale issue tips from "grumbling but staying" into losing the best people, hurting delivery and reputation.
- **Management self-dealing** — capital allocation or comp that favors insiders over owners. *(A live concern; verify in the proxy.)*
- **Your own concentration** — your salary is already a big undiversified bet on Cognizant; buying stock doubles it. If the bear case hits, job and portfolio fall together.

The counter-evidence that keeps the bear case from being a certainty: bookings still ~1.3x and growing, headcount still growing, net cash, buybacks shrinking the count ~3%/yr, and a 2026 selloff that was mostly *sector + sentiment + forced index-removal selling* — i.e., partly technical overshoot, not a Cognizant miss.

## 7. Bottom line — the buy/wait decision

Cognizant fails the Buffett *quality* bar where it counts: the moat is narrow and eroding, the long-term prospects are contested, and management honesty is flagged. It passes decisively on balance sheet, cash generation, and — the seductive part — price. That combination is the definition of a **potential value trap that might also be a real bargain**, and the deciding variable (does AI turn slowing growth into real decline?) is *unresolved*, with the inside evidence leaning cautious.

So the disciplined answer to *"buy now at the lows, or wait for it to drop more?"* is:

> **WAIT — buy only a small starter now, if anything. Don't back up the truck at $43.**

- The reverse-DCF says a lot of bad news is already priced in, so this is *not* an "avoid/sell" — it's not expensive.
- But the moat, management, and unresolved-AI flags mean you should demand a **wide margin of safety** you don't yet have at $43.
- **Waiting is cheap** (you forgo ~3% yield) and the downside scenario (low $30s, near the ~$37 low) is live.
- **Add aggressively on either trigger:** price into the **low $30s** (the margin of safety appears), *or* **2–3 quarters of stable organic revenue + stable pricing + book-to-bill ≥1.0** (the AI thesis de-risks). You rarely get cheap *and* de-risked at once — be patient for whichever comes.
- **Size it small regardless**, because your paycheck is already a large, undiversified long on the same company.

**The enduring lesson, and the reason this pairs with Rover:** Rover proved a wonderful business bought too dear is a mediocre investment. Cognizant tests the mirror — *a cheap price is only a bargain if the cheapness is fear, not decline.* At ~$43 the market is betting on gentle decline; your job is to decide whether reality will be better or worse than that bet, and right now the honest verdict is "wait until the price or the evidence tips the odds clearly in your favor."
