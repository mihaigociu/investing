# Cognizant — Value Investing Analysis Plan (Buffett Style)

> **Objective:** Decide whether Cognizant (Nasdaq: CTSH) is a sound long-term value investment at its current ~$43 price (a multi-year low), and specifically whether to **buy now, wait for a lower price, or avoid it as a value trap**. Unlike the Rover case study, this is a *live* decision with no buyer's "answer key" — the future of the IT-services model under generative AI is the crux and is genuinely unresolved.

> **Status reminder:** All figures are *approximate and illustrative*. **Verify every number against the filings** in `data-sources-and-checklist.md` before acting. The anchor data (FY2025 actuals, FY2026 guidance, ~$43 price) is current as of July 2026.

---

## The philosophy in one paragraph

Buffett-style value investing asks four questions, in order:
1. **Can I understand this business?** (Circle of competence)
2. **Does it have a durable competitive advantage — a moat — that will still be there in 10–20 years?**
3. **Is it run by able, honest managers who allocate capital rationally?**
4. **Is it available at a sensible price with a margin of safety below intrinsic value?**

A "no" (or "can't tell") on 1–3 means you *stop* — price is irrelevant for a business you don't understand or that has no moat. **The special hazard here is question 2.** Cognizant is unmistakably cheap on question 4, which is seductive. But a cheap price for a business whose moat is *eroding* is the definition of a value trap. This plan is structured to make you earn the "buy" by resolving the moat/durability question *first*, before the low multiple is allowed to tempt you.

## Phase 1 — Understand the business (Circle of Competence)

**Deliverable:** a plain-English description a non-investor would understand, plus the unit economics.

Cognizant is an **IT services and consulting** firm. In plain terms: large organizations (banks, insurers, healthcare payers, manufacturers, retailers) hire Cognizant to **build, run, and modernize their technology** — application development and maintenance, cloud migration, data/AI platforms, business-process operations, and increasingly "digital engineering." Roughly **351,600 employees** (most in India-based delivery centers) do the work; clients pay for their time and output.

The economic engine is **labor arbitrage**: bill a client in USD/EUR at an onshore-blended rate, deliver much of the work from lower-cost geographies (India, and increasingly other low-cost hubs), and keep the spread. Layer on **utilization** (keep billable people busy) and the **pyramid** (many juniors under fewer seniors) and you have the classic IT-services margin structure — operating margins in the mid-teens.

- [ ] **Map the revenue engine.** A dollar of revenue = billable hours × bill rate, minus delivery cost. What are the key levers — headcount growth, utilization, pricing, offshore mix, and now **AI-driven productivity**? Which are rising and which are falling?
- [ ] **Segment mix.** Four segments: **Health Sciences, Financial Services, Products & Resources, Communications/Media/Technology.** Which are growing, which shrinking? Health Sciences and Financial Services are the largest.
- [ ] **Contract types.** Time-and-materials (bill per hour — directly exposed to AI cutting hours) vs. fixed-price/managed-services (bill for an outcome — AI productivity *helps* the vendor's margin). The mix shift matters enormously for the AI thesis.
- [ ] **Demand drivers & cyclicality.** Enterprise IT budgets, discretionary "transformation" spend (cyclical, cut first in downturns) vs. non-discretionary "keep-the-lights-on" run work (stickier). How much is which?
- [ ] **The AI question, framed correctly.** GenAI is *both*: (a) a **headwind** — it makes each developer more productive, so a given project needs fewer billable hours (deflationary to a time-and-materials model); and (b) a **tailwind** — enterprises hire Cognizant to *build their AI*, creating new project demand. **The whole investment case is the net of these two.** *(Insider input: see `insider-perspective.md` — the employee view is that AI is "already cannibalizing" billable hours today. Weight this heavily.)*

**Circle-of-competence gate:** Can *you* explain how Cognizant makes money, why a client picks it over a competitor or over doing the work in-house, and what would make it fail? *(As an employee you clear this easily — but write it down honestly; insider familiarity can create blind spots as easily as insight. You see delivery from the inside, not the client's boardroom or the CFO's budget.)*

## Phase 2 — The Moat (Durable Competitive Advantage)

**Deliverable:** a verdict on moat *width* and *durability*, with evidence, not assertions. **This is the decisive phase for this stock.**

Unlike Rover's network effect, an IT-services moat is built from:

- [ ] **Switching costs / embeddedness.** Once Cognizant runs a client's core systems and knows their environment, ripping it out is costly and risky. This is the primary moat. *(Insider input: clients are "sticky but price-squeezed" — they stay, but renegotiate rates hard. So the moat protects **volume** but not **price**. That is a real but **narrow** moat.)*
- [ ] **Scale & delivery capability.** Global delivery network, security clearances, industry certifications, the ability to staff a 2,000-person program fast. Hard for a small rival to replicate.
- [ ] **Long-term contracts & backlog.** Multi-year managed-services deals create revenue visibility (see bookings/book-to-bill).
- [ ] **Reputation / relationships.** Trusted-vendor status, reference-ability, existing relationships that make renewals the default.
- [ ] **Cost position.** Scaled offshore delivery. But this is a moat *shared* by every large Indian/global peer — it protects the industry, not Cognizant specifically.

**Moat stress tests (be adversarial — this is where value traps hide):**
- [ ] **AI disintermediation of the labor model.** The structural threat: if GenAI lets a client's own staff (or a small AI-native vendor) do what took 100 Cognizant engineers, the labor-arbitrage spread compresses or disappears. *This is the ROVR "disintermediation" analog — and the insider says it's already happening.*
- [ ] **Pricing power.** A real moat lets you *raise price*. Cognizant is being asked to *lower* price (pass AI savings to clients). A moat that can't hold price is narrow and possibly eroding.
- [ ] **Commoditization.** Is Cognizant differentiated, or one of ~6 interchangeable large vendors (Accenture, TCS, Infosys, Wipro, HCL, Capgemini, IBM) competing largely on price? Historically CTSH has been a *share loser* and margin laggard vs. Accenture and the top Indian names.
- [ ] **Insourcing.** Cheap AI tooling lowers the cost for clients to do the work themselves.

**Moat verdict:** rate it **None / Narrow / Wide**, and **Widening / Stable / Eroding**. *Draft: **Narrow and Eroding** — real switching costs on the installed base, but no pricing power and a core model under active AI compression. A narrow-but-eroding moat is the classic value-trap profile; it must be bought with a wide margin of safety or not at all.*

## Phase 3 — Management & Capital Allocation

**Deliverable:** a judgment on operating ability and — the decisive test — capital-allocation rationality and honesty.

- [ ] **Operating track record.** CEO **Ravi Kumar S** (joined Jan 2023, from Infosys) stabilized growth: revenue reaccelerated to +7% in 2025 after years of ~2% laggard growth, with four straight quarters of organic YoY growth and margins holding ~16%. Give credit where due — that is a genuine operational improvement.
- [ ] **Candor.** How plainly do they discuss the AI threat to their *own* model, vs. spinning AI purely as an opportunity? Watch the balance of GAAP vs. "adjusted" metrics (the adjusted-vs-GAAP gap is modest for CTSH — a quality point vs. Rover).
- [ ] **Capital allocation — the decisive test.**
  - **Buybacks:** ~$1.3B in 2025 (~17.4M shares), ~$1B planned for 2026. At ~8x FCF this is *value-accretive* if the business merely stagnates — arguably management's best current use of cash, and a signal they think the stock is cheap.
  - **Dividend:** ~$1.32/yr (~3% yield), raised 6.5% in 2026; ~29% payout — comfortably covered.
  - **Acquisitions:** history of tuck-in M&A (e.g., Belcan/engineering, Thirdera, etc.). Value-accretive or empire-building? Check returns.
- [ ] **Insider ownership & incentives.** Do the top executives own meaningful stock? Are comp targets tied to *per-share* value and returns on capital, or to revenue/headcount growth and "adjusted" metrics that flatter optics? **Read the DEF 14A carefully.**
- [ ] **The honesty red flag (insider input).** The employee view: most staff had **salary increases skipped**, while **senior management "got their share."** Two readings, and you must weigh both:
  - *Charitable:* disciplined cost control in a tough market protects margins and buyback capacity — good for shareholders.
  - *Damning:* management is **extracting** — protecting its own comp and short-term optics on the backs of the workforce, which is (a) a Buffett-level governance/candor red flag, and (b) a slow-burn risk to delivery quality and attrition. *(Insider read leans toward "extracting" — this materially caps the management score. See `insider-perspective.md`.)*
- [ ] **Stock-based compensation.** Quantify SBC as a % of revenue and treat it as a real cost in owner earnings (it's moderate for CTSH, but real).

## Phase 4 — Financial Strength & Earnings Power

**Deliverable:** a quality-of-business scorecard and a defensible estimate of **normalized owner earnings**.

- [ ] **Growth & its quality.** Revenue CAGR and, crucially, **constant-currency organic** growth (strip out FX and acquisitions). 2025's +7% (+6.4% cc) reversed years of ~2% stagnation — is it durable or a one-off?
- [ ] **Profitability & margins.** Adjusted operating margin ~16%, expanding modestly. Note margin has been a *laggard* vs. Accenture (~15%+) and especially the top Indian firms (TCS/Infosys ~20–25%). Can AI-driven productivity lift margins even as it deflates revenue? (The bull case for margins; watch it.)
- [ ] **Returns on capital.** Services firms are capital-light (capex ~1–1.5% of revenue), so ROIC/ROE are structurally high. Compute ROIC *excluding excess cash and goodwill distortion*. High ROIC is the quantitative signature of a moat — but check whether *incremental* returns are holding as growth slows.
- [ ] **Owner earnings (Buffett's metric).**
  `Owner earnings ≈ Net income + D&A + other non-cash − maintenance capex − normalized WC change − real SBC.`
  For CTSH, FCF (~$2.67B in 2025, ~120% of net income) is already close to owner earnings; subtract the (small, 10-K-actual) SBC of ~$181M → **~$2.48B**, roughly **$5.24/share.** Against ~$43, that's **~8.2x owner earnings.**
- [ ] **Free cash flow & conversion.** FCF conversion >100% of net income is a genuine quality signal — real cash, not accounting profit.
- [ ] **Balance sheet & solvency.** ~$1.9B cash/investments vs. ~$0.58B debt = **net cash ~$1.3B.** No solvency risk; survives a long downturn and funds buybacks without stress. A clear Buffett positive.
- [ ] **Earnings consistency.** Unlike Rover, CTSH has a **20+ year public record** through multiple cycles — a real advantage for a philosophy that wants a decade of proof. The caveat: the *AI era* has no precedent in that record.

## Phase 5 — Intrinsic Value & Margin of Safety

**Deliverable:** a *range* of intrinsic value per share, and the price at which CTSH becomes a buy. (Use `valuation-worksheet.md`.)

- [ ] **Reverse DCF — do this one FIRST; it's the most honest.** At ~$43 (EV ~$19B, FCF ~$2.5–2.7B ≈ ~7x EV/FCF), what does the price *already assume*? Answer (see worksheet): the market is pricing roughly **perpetual ~3–4%/year FCF decline.** That reframes everything: **you don't need growth to make money here — you need the decline to be *milder than ~4%/year.*** That is the crux of the bull case.
- [ ] **Owner-earnings / FCF DCF.** Project 10 years under three cases; discount at ~10% (higher than a blue-chip staple, to reflect real disruption risk); modest terminal growth (or terminal *decline* in the bear case).
  - **Bear (AI guts the model):** owner earnings decline ~5–8%/yr → intrinsic ~**$30–35 or below.**
  - **Base (stagnation, flat-to-slightly-down):** ~**$50–55.**
  - **Bull (transition works; low-single-digit growth + buybacks compound):** ~**$65–75.**
- [ ] **Multiples sanity-check.** EV/Revenue ~0.9x, P/E ~8x, P/FCF ~8x — all roughly *half* the 10-year medians (~18x). Cross-check against peers (`peers-and-benchmark.md`): CTSH is the cheapest of the majors, which is either the opportunity or the market's verdict on relative quality.
- [ ] **Normalize.** Strip FX, one-off tax items, and acquisition contributions to find *through-cycle organic* earnings power.
- [ ] **Apply a margin of safety.** Given a **narrow-and-eroding moat**, a **management-honesty flag**, and a **structural (not cyclical) threat**, demand a *wide* margin — **35–40%** off the base case. On a ~$52 base, that's a buy price around **$32–34.**

**Then answer the live decision:**
- [ ] **Buy now, wait, or avoid?** Compare ~$43 to your intrinsic range and your margin-of-safety buy price. If $43 > buy price but < base case, the honest answer is usually *"cheap but not yet a margin of safety — wait or buy small."*
- [ ] **The staged / tranche plan.** Because you can't time the bottom and the thesis is genuinely uncertain, consider *tranches* tied to price **and** to evidence (see worksheet §6): a starter now only if you must, more as either the price falls into the low $30s **or** the revenue line demonstrably holds through the AI transition (2–3 quarters of stable organic growth + stable pricing + book-to-bill ≥1.0).

## Phase 6 — Pre-Mortem: Risks & Disconfirming Evidence

**Deliverable:** the strongest possible *bear* case, written by you against your own thesis. For a cheap stock, the pre-mortem *is* the analysis — cheapness is only a bargain if the bear case is wrong.

- [ ] **AI structurally deflates the labor model** — the thesis-killer. Fewer billable hours per outcome, and clients demanding the savings. *(Insider says this is already underway.)* If revenue turns to sustained *decline*, the 8x multiple is not cheap — it's correct, and it will fall further.
- [ ] **Pricing power loss** — "sticky but price-squeezed" means margins get ground down over years even if revenue holds.
- [ ] **Discretionary-spend recession** — enterprise transformation budgets are cyclical; a macro downturn stacks on top of the AI headwind.
- [ ] **Competitive share loss** — CTSH has historically lagged Accenture and top Indian peers on growth and margin. Does it lose share in the AI transition too?
- [ ] **Talent / delivery erosion** — the salary-freeze morale issue ("grumbling but staying") could tip into attrition of the best people, hurting delivery and reputation. *(Not acute yet per insider — watch it.)*
- [ ] **Management extracts value** — capital allocation or comp that favors insiders over shareholders. *(A live concern per insider.)*
- [ ] **The value-trap pattern** — cheap multiple that stays cheap or gets cheaper as earnings slowly decline; you collect a 3% dividend while losing 5%/yr of capital. Indian IT peers already down ~50% from peaks show this can run further.
- [ ] **Falsify your moat claim.** For the switching-cost moat you assert in Phase 2, write the evidence that would disprove it (accelerating client losses, book-to-bill falling below 1.0, pricing declines in disclosures) and check whether it's appearing.

3. **Able, honest, rational management?** Operating: improved. Honesty/owner-orientation: flagged. *(The decisive soft spot.)*
4. **Financial strength & real earnings power?** Strong: net cash, high FCF conversion, high ROIC.
5. **Intrinsic value range** and **your buy price** (with a wide margin of safety).
6. **Buy now / wait / avoid** — and the tranche plan and the specific evidence that would change the call.

**Bottom line:** State plainly whether CTSH at ~$43 is *cheap enough to compensate for a narrow, eroding moat and a management you don't fully trust.* Distinguish **"statistically cheap"** from **"margin of safety."** A cheap stock in structural decline is a value trap; a cheap stock merely suffering cyclical/sentiment fear is the bargain of the decade. The reverse-DCF says the market has *already* priced in decline — so the real question is not "is it cheap?" (it is) but **"is the decline going to be worse than the ~4%/year the price already assumes?"** Your insider evidence is the best tool you have to answer that, and right now it leans cautious.

## Appendix — The Buffett/Munger checklist (quick reference)

- Simple, understandable business within your circle of competence.
- Durable, ideally widening, competitive moat. **← the weak link for CTSH**
- Consistent operating history and predictable earnings.
- High and sustainable returns on invested capital.
- Little debt / strong balance sheet. **← a clear CTSH strength**
- Honest, able, owner-oriented management with a good capital-allocation record. **← flagged for CTSH**
- Favorable long-term prospects (secular tailwind, long runway). **← contested: AI is both tailwind and headwind**
- A rational estimate of intrinsic value.
- A price offering a **margin of safety** below that value. **← the seductive part; don't let it override the moat question**
- The temperament to buy when fearful, hold for years, and ignore quotes — *but not to confuse a falling knife for a fearful market.*

*"Our favorite holding period is forever" — but only for businesses that clear every item above. Cognizant clears the price and balance-sheet items easily and stumbles on the moat and management items — which is exactly why the price is low.*
