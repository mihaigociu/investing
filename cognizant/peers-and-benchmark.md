# Peers, the 2026 Sector Selloff & the "No Answer Key" Benchmark — Cognizant

Rover had a clean answer key: Blackstone's $11 buyout, blessed by two banks' fairness opinions. **Cognizant has none — no buyer, no fairness opinion, just a live market price and a genuinely uncertain future.** So this file assembles the next-best external checks:
1. **The 2026 sector selloff** — what actually crashed the stock (mostly *not* Cognizant-specific).
2. **Peer comps** — where CTSH sits vs. Accenture and the Indian majors.
3. **Analysts vs. the reverse-DCF** — two very different "answer keys," and why the second is more honest.

---

## Part 1 — Why the stock fell ~48% in 2026 (it's mostly the sector)

Cognizant did not miss. FY2025 was its best year in a while (+7% revenue, +11% adjusted EPS), and it kept its 2026 guidance. The crash was **external and sentiment-driven**, a stack of overlapping blows to the *whole IT-services sector*:

| Trigger (2026) | What happened | Why it hit CTSH |
|---|---|---|
| **Accenture guidance cut** | The bellwether (ACN — FY2025 revenue $69.7B, +7%) cut its **FY26 revenue-growth guide to 3–4% local currency** on a Q3-FY26 print with softer bookings and deals pushed to FY27; the stock fell ~18% in a day (a record drop) and is **−49% YTD in 2026** (~$259 → ~$130). | ACN is the sector tell. Its warning re-rated *every* outsourcing/consulting name lower on fears of a demand air-pocket. |
| **GenAI disruption fear** | Narrative that "spending on AI tokens and cloud is taking away from traditional IT services," and that AI could gut the labor-arbitrage model. | CTSH *is* a labor-arbitrage model — squarely in the crosshairs. |
| **Indian IT collapse** | TCS, Infosys, Wipro, LTIMindtree all down ~50% from peaks; Infosys at a 5-year low, TCS near a 6-year low. JPMorgan: sector "stuck at 2–3% growth for 3 years," recovery unclear. | Same business model; confirms this is a *sector* de-rating, not a CTSH stumble. |
| **Berenberg downgrade** | Cut CTSH to Hold, lower target, citing AI-transition risk to traditional outsourcing/consulting. | Reinforced the bear narrative at a fragile moment. |
| **Nasdaq-100 removal** | CTSH removed from the index effective 22 June 2026 → forced technical selling by index funds. | Mechanical, non-fundamental selling pressure — the kind that can *overshoot* fair value (a potential opportunity signal). |

> **What this tells a value investor:** the decline is **sector + sentiment + technical**, not a fresh deterioration in Cognizant's own results. That is *exactly* the "be greedy when others are fearful" setup Buffett prizes — **IF** the underlying business is merely being over-feared. The whole question is whether the fear is *wrong* (opportunity) or *early* (value trap). The index-removal forced-selling in particular argues part of the drop is technical overshoot. But "the peers fell too" is not proof the fear is wrong — the peers may all be correctly priced for a shared structural decline.

## Part 2 — Peer comparison (filing-exact, most recent fiscal year)

CTSH is one of ~6–7 large, substitutable IT-services vendors. The peer set both *validates the cheapness* (whole sector de-rated) and *frames the relative-quality question* (CTSH is the cheapest — deservedly or not?).

> ⚠️ **Fiscal years differ — compare with care.** Accenture's FY ends **August** (FY2025 = yr to Aug-2025); CTSH's ends **December** (FY2025 = yr to Dec-2025); the Indian majors' end **March** (FY2026 = yr to Mar-2026). "Rev growth (cc)" is **constant-currency** — the most comparable growth measure. Revenue is USD.

| Company (latest FY) | Revenue | Rev growth (cc) | Op. margin | P/E (fwd/current) | 2026 stock | Div. yield |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| **Cognizant (CTSH)** | $21.1B *(FY25)* | **+6.4%** | 16.1% GAAP (15.8% adj) | **~7.6x fwd** | **−48% YTD** | ~3.0% |
| **Accenture (ACN)** | $69.7B *(FY25)* | +7% *(FY25)*; **FY26 guide +3–4%** | 14.7% GAAP (15.6% adj) | ~9x fwd | **−49% YTD** | ~4.8% |
| **TCS** | >$30B *(FY26)* | **−2.4%** | **25.0%** | ~15–17x | near 6-yr low | ~3–4% |
| **Infosys (INFY)** | $20.2B *(FY26)* | +3.1% | 20.3% IFRS (21.0% adj) | ~16x | 5-yr low | ~2–3% |
| **HCLTech** | $14.7B *(FY26)* | +3.9% | 17.2% (17.9% ex-restr.) | ~low-teens | de-rated | ~5.6% |
| **Wipro (WIT)** | $10.5B *(FY26)* | +1.6% | 17.2% | ~14x | de-rated | ~2–3% |

**What the comps say:**
- **CTSH is the cheapest major** (~7.6x forward vs. Accenture ~9x and the India-listed names ~14–17x), *and* — the surprise — it posted the **best constant-currency growth of the entire group** in its latest year (+6.4%, vs. Infosys +3.1%, HCLTech +3.9%, Wipro +1.6%, Accenture's FY26 guide +3–4%, and **TCS actually −2.4%**). So the reflexive "CTSH is the lowest-quality laggard, deservedly cheapest" story is *contradicted by the most recent data.* Historically CTSH did lag on growth and share; right now it is out-growing all of them and priced the cheapest — which is either a genuine mispricing or the market betting the +6.4% is a one-year head-fake that reverts.
- **The margin gap is real but CTSH is mid-pack, not bottom.** CTSH's ~16% operating margin beats Accenture (14.7% GAAP) and matches Wipro/HCLTech (~17%), but trails Infosys (~21%) and TCS (~25%) — the top Indian names run higher offshore mix and thus fatter margins. If AI productivity lets CTSH hold its ~$10B fixed-price book at current prices (see `contract-mix-and-ai-exposure.md`), there's margin-expansion room; if pricing is competed away, the gap persists.
- **The value-trap risk is not hypothetical — TCS proves it.** The best-run, highest-margin (25%) Indian major posted a **−2.4% constant-currency revenue decline** in FY2026. That is the sector's demand/AI headwind already showing up as *actual revenue contraction* at the strongest operator — concrete evidence the bear case can be real, not just feared. It's the single most sobering data point in the peer set and the reason "cheap" must be stress-tested against "declining."
- **Accenture is a competing value candidate — weigh it.** The premium leader fell just as hard (−49%), trades ~9x forward with a **~4.8% yield** (vs. CTSH's ~3%), has more consulting/higher-value mix, and is far larger/more diversified. If your thesis is "the sector is over-feared," the cleaner expression may be the *higher-quality* leader at a similar multiple, not the cheapest name. CTSH's edge over ACN is price (~7.6x vs ~9x) and faster recent growth; ACN's edge is quality, scale, and yield. A disciplined investor compares the two directly rather than defaulting to CTSH because it's the employer.

> **The Rover analogy, inverted.** For Rover, the peer (Wag!) traded at ~1x sales vs. Rover's ~9x — the market pricing Rover's *superiority*. Here, the whole peer group trades cheap and CTSH cheapest of all — the market pricing the *sector's shared existential question.* But unlike the Rover-vs-Wag! gap, CTSH is **not** the obvious quality laggard on current numbers (it out-grew the group). Cheap-vs-peers still isn't a margin of safety if the peers are cheap for a real reason — and TCS's −2.4% shows the reason has teeth.

## Part 3 — Two "answer keys": analysts vs. the reverse-DCF

With no acquirer's price, the closest things to an answer key are (a) analyst intrinsic-value estimates and (b) what the market price itself implies.

### Analyst estimates — treat skeptically

| Source | Estimate | Implied vs. ~$43 |
|---|---|---|
| GuruFocus "GF Value" | ~$84 | ~+95% |
| Sell-side bull case ("$67 by 2028") | ~$67 by 2028 | ~+55% over 2+ yrs |
| Berenberg (post-downgrade) | Hold, reduced target | roughly in line / modest upside |

Most of these are anchored to **pre-disruption multiples** (~18x historical P/E). Mechanically re-applying an 18x multiple to a business the market now fears is structurally impaired will *always* say "cheap." That is precisely the value-trap error. **Don't lean on these.**

### The reverse-DCF — the honest answer key

Instead of asserting a multiple, ask what the ~$43 price *already assumes* (full math in `valuation-worksheet.md` §3):

- EV ~$19B ÷ FCF ~$2.5–2.7B ≈ **~7x EV/FCF → ~13% FCF yield.**
- A no-growth perpetuity at a 10% discount would be worth ~10x FCF. At ~7x, the market is implying FCF **declines ~3–4%/year in perpetuity.**

> **This is the single most useful sentence in the whole analysis:** *At ~$43, you don't need Cognizant to grow — you need its cash flow to decline by less than ~3–4% per year.* If the business stagnates (flat), you're materially underpaying. If it grows even low-single-digits, the stock is worth $60–75. If AI drives a *faster* structural decline (5–8%/yr — the genuine bear case your insider view keeps alive), then ~$43 is not cheap; it's roughly fair, with more downside.

So the two answer keys disagree sharply, and that disagreement *is* the investment:
- **Analysts** (anchored to the past): worth ~$67–84 → screaming buy.
- **Reverse-DCF** (anchored to the price): the market already expects gentle decline → cheap only if decline is milder than that.

The reverse-DCF wins because it doesn't require you to forecast — it just makes the market's bet explicit so you can decide whether you'd take the other side.

---

## Bottom line from the benchmarks

> The 2026 crash was a **sector-wide, sentiment-and-technical de-rating**, not a Cognizant miss — the classic contour of a Buffett opportunity. CTSH is now the **cheapest of the large IT-services majors**, on ~8x earnings and ~7x FCF, having just posted the group's best growth. **But** the peers are cheap for a shared, real reason (AI's threat to labor arbitrage), CTSH has historically been the group's *quality laggard*, and the reverse-DCF shows the price already bakes in gentle decline. The benchmark verdict: *cheap enough to be interesting, not obviously cheap enough to be safe.* Whether ~$43 is a bargain or a trap comes down to the moat/AI judgment in the plan — and that's where your insider read (`insider-perspective.md`) does the heavy lifting.
