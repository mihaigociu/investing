# Insider Perspective — The Employee-View Annex

This analysis has something most don't: the analyst **works at Cognizant.** That's a real edge on the *qualitative* Buffett questions (moat durability, morale, whether AI is *actually* cutting billable hours) — the things filings lag by quarters or obscure entirely. It's also a hazard: proximity breeds bias. This file records the inside inputs, maps each to the scorecard, and — critically — **stress-tests each one against the risk that it's distorted.**

> ⚠️ **How to use insider knowledge legally and well.** Use it for *qualitative texture and mosaic judgment* (morale, adoption, direction) built from ordinary work experience — not for trading on **material non-public information** (unannounced results, deals, guidance). If you ever hold specific MNPI, the correct action is to *not trade* until it's public, and to follow Cognizant's insider-trading/blackout policy. Everything below is general, experiential, and directional — the kind of "scuttlebutt" Phil Fisher and Buffett prize — not a leak of the next print.

## The four inside reads and what they mean

### 1. AI is "already cannibalizing" billable work → **the bear case is real, not hypothetical**
**Input:** On real engagements, GenAI tools are measurably reducing the people/hours a project needs — today, not in some future.

**Why it's the most important input in the whole analysis:** The entire market debate is whether AI structurally deflates the labor-arbitrage model. Outsiders are *guessing* from Accenture's guidance and sector sentiment. You are *seeing it happen* on the delivery floor. That the deflation is already underway, from the inside, is powerful corroboration of the market's fear — and it's what pushes the moat-trend score to "eroding" (#3) and keeps the reverse-DCF's bear scenario (>4%/yr decline) firmly alive rather than a strawman.

**Stress-test (is this read biased?):**
- *Over-pessimism risk:* engineers see the hours cut on *their* project but not the *new AI-build projects* the sales org is winning elsewhere. The firm-level net could still be positive even if your corner feels the cannibalization. Bookings ~1.3x and headcount *still growing* (+14,800 in 2025) are the counter-evidence — reconcile them with what you see.
- *Timing:* "fewer hours per project" hurts time-and-materials revenue but can *help* margins on fixed-price work. Which contract type dominates what you observe?
- **Net judgment:** treat as strong evidence the headwind is real, but not proof it's *winning* firm-wide. It raises the probability of the bear case; it doesn't confirm it. Watch revenue-per-employee and organic growth to see if the firm-level numbers catch up to the floor-level experience.

### 2. "Grumbling but staying" after skipped raises → **near-term cost/margin support, long-term talent risk**
**Input:** Most employees didn't get salary increases; morale and resentment are high, but attrition is manageable and delivery is holding.

**What it means for the numbers:** Skipping raises is **direct margin support** — labor is the main cost, so a wage freeze props up the ~16% margin and the buyback capacity in the short run. That's part of *why* 2025 margins expanded. Good for this year's shareholder, in isolation.

**Stress-test / the catch:**
- A wage freeze is a **one-time lever, not a repeatable one.** You can't freeze pay every year without eventually losing your best people. It flatters current margins in a way that isn't sustainable — don't extrapolate the margin gain.
- "Grumbling but staying" is fine *until it isn't.* In a soft IT job market (peers also cutting), people stay because there's nowhere to go — a macro accident, not loyalty. If hiring recovers, the pent-up attrition could release at once.
- **Net judgment:** mildly positive for near-term margins/EPS (already in the numbers), a slow-burn risk to delivery quality and the moat. Keeps management-operating at a 3 (it worked) but feeds the management-honesty concern (#8, below).

### 3. Clients are "sticky but price-squeezed" → **the moat protects volume, not price**
**Input:** Clients don't leave (switching costs are real), but they aggressively renegotiate rates and demand that AI savings be passed to them.

**Why this is the sharpest moat insight:** A Buffett moat is fundamentally about **pricing power** — "the single most important decision in evaluating a business is pricing power" (Buffett). A business whose customers can't leave *but can dictate price* has a **narrow** moat: it defends revenue *volume* but surrenders the economics over time via margin erosion. That's exactly the "sticky but price-squeezed" profile, and it's why moat width is a 2, not a 4. The switching costs are real (keeps #2 and #3 off a 1) but they're being *monetized away by the client, not the company.*

**Stress-test:**
- Some rate pressure is normal in IT services every cycle; the question is whether it's *worse* now (AI-justified) than the usual haggling. If it's structurally worse, margins grind down for years (bear). If it's cyclical toughness, it passes.
- Fixed-price/outcome-based contracts flip this: if Cognizant can hold an outcome price while AI cuts its delivery cost, pricing pressure becomes a *margin tailwind.* The contract-mix shift is the thing to watch.

### 4. Management is "extracting" → **the near-veto governance flag**
**Input:** Senior management is seen as prioritizing its own compensation and short-term optics — protecting the top while freezing pay for the rank and file.

**Why Buffett weights this heavily:** Buffett's third pillar is *honest, owner-oriented management.* A team that takes care of itself while squeezing the workforce fails the "would I want them as a partner?" test — and it corrodes the very asset (skilled people) that *is* an IT-services company. This is what caps management-honesty at a 2 and holds the whole scorecard back despite the cheap price. It's the difference between "cheap because misunderstood" (buy) and "cheap because the stewards aren't trustworthy" (pass).

**Stress-test (be fair to management):**
- Freezing pay in a demand-uncertain, AI-threatened year is *defensible cost discipline*, and returning $2B to shareholders (buyback at ~8x FCF + a raised dividend) is *rational, pro-shareholder* capital allocation. As a *shareholder*, some of what feels like "extraction" from the *employee* seat is management doing its job for owners.
- The genuine red flag isn't cost discipline — it's the *asymmetry* (top protected, staff not) and whether comp is tied to per-share value or to self-serving "adjusted" metrics. **Verify in the DEF 14A** before hardening this to a veto. Check Form 4s: are executives *buying* at $43 (aligned) or only selling vested grants (extractive)?
- **Net judgment:** a real ding, appropriately near-veto, but *confirm it with the proxy* rather than resting on floor sentiment. Employee-seat frustration and shareholder-seat interests genuinely diverge here — hold both in view.

## The employee/shareholder conflict — name it explicitly

You are evaluating this from **two seats at once**, and they don't fully align:

| | As an **employee** | As a **shareholder** |
|---|---|---|
| Skipped raises | Bad (your pay, morale) | Good short-term (margin, buyback) |
| Cost discipline | Feels like extraction | Rational capital allocation |
| AI cannibalization | Threatens your job | Threatens your capital *too* |
| Buying more CTSH | — | **Concentrates** both risks in one bet |

**The concentration point is the one that matters most for you personally.** Your salary is already a large, undiversified long position in Cognizant. If the AI-disruption bear case is right, your pay *and* your portfolio suffer together — the worst kind of correlation. Even where the stock analysis says "cheap," prudent sizing for *you* is **smaller than for an outside investor**, and this reinforces the "wait / buy only small" verdict on its own, independent of valuation.
